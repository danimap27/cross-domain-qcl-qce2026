\begin{abstract}
Sequential task training in parameterized quantum circuits produces
parameter interference across tasks, a phenomenon known as catastrophic
forgetting. This paper proposes cross-domain pre-training as a
memory-free strategy to reduce this interference: a hybrid
classical-quantum model is trained on a synthetic Gaussian dataset
before exposure to the target task sequence, placing circuit parameters
in a more stable region of parameter space. Three circuit architectures
are evaluated---Strongly Entangling Layers, Basic Entangler, and Tree
Tensor Networks---under both ideal simulation and a realistic noise
model derived from IBM Heron~r2 hardware calibration data, using
amplitude damping, phase damping, and depolarizing channels. Experiments
are executed on the Hercules HPC cluster over five random seeds and show
that synthetic pre-training reduces the forgetting drop on the first
task by more than 25\% relative to random initialization, without
requiring replay buffers or parameter regularization. Tree Tensor
Network circuits produce the lowest forgetting drop in both noise
conditions, consistent with their hierarchical parameter isolation.
Under hardware noise, all architectures degrade in absolute accuracy,
but the relative ordering across architectures and initialization
strategies is preserved.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Quantum Machine Learning (QML) models based on Variational Quantum
Circuits (VQC) have attracted research interest as candidate algorithms
for near-term quantum hardware operating in the Noisy
Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) regime~\cite{preskill2018quantum}.
A VQC encodes classical data into a parameterized quantum state and
applies a sequence of trainable unitary gates optimized by a classical
gradient-based method~\cite{cerezo2021variational,mitarai2018quantum}.
Binary classification with VQCs has been studied across image
recognition tasks~\cite{schuld2019evaluating}, where dimensionality
reduction methods map high-dimensional inputs into the low-dimensional
space accessible to near-term hardware.

One open challenge in deploying VQC models in practice is Quantum
Continual Learning (QCL): the scenario where the same circuit must
process tasks arriving in sequence without access to prior task data.
When a VQC trained on Task~A is subsequently trained on Task~B, the
gradient updates for Task~B overwrite the parameters learned for
Task~A, causing a sharp drop in Task~A accuracy. This effect, termed
Catastrophic Forgetting (CF), has been studied in classical neural
networks~\cite{parisi2019continual,kirkpatrick2017overcoming}, but its
behavior in parameterized quantum circuits is less characterized,
particularly under realistic hardware noise.

The standard approaches to CF in classical models fall into two
categories: regularization methods that constrain the gradient of
previously important parameters~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming}, and
replay methods that store past task samples~\cite{buzzega2020dark}.
Both carry costs that conflict with the resource constraints of NISQ
devices: regularization requires computing and storing Fisher
information matrices at scale, while replay buffers demand memory that
grows with task count. A third approach, explored in classical transfer
learning~\cite{pan2009survey}, is to initialize model parameters from
a pre-trained source domain, placing the initial configuration in a
region of parameter space that resists task-specific overfitting.

Quantum Transfer Learning (QTL) has been studied as a method for
accelerating convergence in VQC classification
tasks~\cite{mari2020transfer}. Its effect on sequential forgetting has
not been directly assessed. The specific question addressed in this
paper is whether pre-training a VQC on a synthetic Gaussian source
domain---one with no semantic overlap with the target image
tasks---produces a parameter initialization that reduces CF during
subsequent sequential training, and whether this effect holds under
realistic NISQ noise.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it formalizes the
cross-domain QCL setup, where the source domain is a synthetic
Gaussian dataset and the target sequence consists of binary image
classification tasks drawn from Fashion-MNIST~\cite{xiao2017fashion}
and MNIST~\cite{lecun1998gradient}. Second, it conducts a systematic
comparison of three VQC ans\"{a}tze---Strongly Entangling Layers
(SEL), Basic Entangler, and Tree Tensor Network (TTN)---under both
ideal and noisy simulation conditions, using a hardware-calibrated
noise model from IBM~Heron~r2. Third, it evaluates three
initialization strategies (random, synthetic Gaussian, and
MobileNet-V2~\cite{howard2017mobilenets} feature pre-training) to
isolate the source of the forgetting reduction.

\section{Related Work}

This section reviews the three areas of literature that intersect in
this work: classical approaches to CF, VQC training challenges, and
noise in near-term quantum devices.

\subsection{Catastrophic Forgetting in Neural Models}

CF arises when gradient-based optimization on a new task shifts
parameters away from the minima established for prior
tasks~\cite{parisi2019continual}. Elastic Weight Consolidation
(EWC)~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming} computes a Fisher information
approximation to identify parameters critical to previous tasks and
applies a quadratic penalty to constrain their movement during new
task training. Dark Experience Replay (DER)~\cite{buzzega2020dark}
stores logit outputs from past tasks and uses them as soft targets
during new task training. Both methods have classical counterparts that
do not transfer directly to quantum circuits, since quantum expectation
values have a periodic dependence on circuit parameters that changes
the geometry of the loss landscape. Lifelong machine
learning~\cite{chen2018catastrophic} provides a broader taxonomy of CF
mitigation strategies, but few have been adapted for the quantum
setting.

\subsection{Variational Quantum Circuits and Barren Plateaus}

A VQC produces a scalar output through the expectation value of an
observable over a parameterized quantum state. The training landscape
of VQCs is known to suffer from barren plateaus: regions where the
gradient variance decreases exponentially with the number of
qubits~\cite{mcclean2018barren,cerezo2021cost}. The TTN architecture
avoids global entanglement by restricting interactions to neighboring
qubit pairs, which reduces the severity of barren
plateaus~\cite{pesah2021absence}. SEL circuits generate higher
entanglement and expressibility~\cite{sim2019expressibility} at the
cost of a larger parameter space and greater susceptibility to gradient
concentration. These structural differences become relevant in the
sequential setting, where circuits that concentrate gradients early
also disrupt previously learned representations more quickly.

\subsection{Quantum Transfer Learning}

Mari et al.~\cite{mari2020transfer} demonstrated that freezing
classical feature extraction layers of a hybrid model and fine-tuning
only the quantum circuit allows training on small datasets with
competitive accuracy. The key finding is that a circuit initialized
from a pre-trained state converges faster than one initialized randomly.
This paper extends that observation to the sequential setting, where
the primary metric is not convergence speed on the target task but
parameter stability across task transitions.

\subsection{Noise in NISQ Devices}

Physical quantum gates introduce decoherence, gate errors, and readout
noise that alter the effective circuit unitary. Wang et
al.~\cite{wang2024quantum} showed that hardware noise can induce its
own form of barren plateau, flattening the training landscape beyond
what isolated error rates would suggest. Under coherence-limited noise
models combining amplitude damping and phase damping, the circuit depth
that can be trained before losing gradient signal is substantially
reduced. This effect is relevant to QCL because noisy circuits not
only reduce absolute accuracy but also alter the shape of the loss
landscape during sequential task transitions, potentially changing the
degree of forgetting relative to ideal conditions.

classical-quantum model receives fixed-length feature vectors and
produces a binary classification output through a parameterized quantum
circuit. The continual learning protocol trains the model on two
sequential tasks without any replay mechanism. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}
shows the overall structure of the pipeline.

\subsection{Data Encoding}

All input data is reduced to four scalar features by Principal
Component Analysis (PCA)~\cite{pearson1901lines} applied to the
training split of each task independently. The four PCA components are
then scaled to $[0, \pi]$ by min-max normalization. These four values
serve as rotation angles for $R_y$ gates at the circuit input:

\begin{equation}
  \ket{\phi(x)} = \bigotimes_{k=1}^{n} R_y(x_k)\ket{0},
  \label{eq:encoding}
\end{equation}

where $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in [0,\pi]^n$ is the scaled feature
vector and $n = 4$ is the number of qubits. This encoding maps each
classical real-valued feature linearly into a rotation angle on the
Bloch sphere, making the input representation compatible across tasks
with different original dimensionalities.

\subsection{Circuit Architectures}

Three ans\"{a}tze are evaluated, all applied after the encoding block of
Eq.~(\ref{eq:encoding}) and followed by a measurement of
$\langle Z_0 \rangle$.

\textit{Strongly Entangling Layers (SEL).} Each layer applies
single-qubit $R_y$ and $R_z$ rotations on all qubits, followed by a
ring of CNOT gates with general connectivity~\cite{sim2019expressibility}.
For $n = 4$ qubits and $L = 2$ layers, the parameter tensor has shape
$(2, 4, 3)$, giving 24 parameters. SEL is the most expressive of the
three architectures tested and generates full $n$-qubit entanglement.

\textit{Basic Entangler.} Each layer applies single-qubit $R_x$
rotations followed by a fixed nearest-neighbor CNOT chain. For $n = 4$
and $L = 2$, the parameter shape is $(2, 4)$, giving 8 parameters. The
lower parametrization limits expressibility but reduces the gradient
concentration associated with wide-entanglement circuits.

\textit{Tree Tensor Network (TTN).} The TTN applies a hierarchical
entanglement structure that limits interactions to neighboring qubit
pairs at each level. For $n = 4$ qubits, the circuit is:

\begin{equation}
  U_\text{TTN}(\theta) =
  U_{1,3}(\theta_4, \theta_5)
  \cdot U_{2,3}(\theta_2, \theta_3)
  \cdot U_{0,1}(\theta_0, \theta_1),
  \label{eq:ttn}
\end{equation}

where $U_{i,j}(\theta_a, \theta_b) = \text{CNOT}(i \to j) \cdot
R_y(\theta_b, j) \cdot R_y(\theta_a, i)$. The total parameter count is
6. The TTN avoids long-range entanglement, which reduces barren plateau
effects~\cite{pesah2021absence,grant2018hierarchical} and, as shown in
Section~\ref{sec:topology}, also limits the propagation of
task-specific gradient updates across the circuit.

\subsection{Continual Learning Protocol}

The sequential training protocol proceeds as follows. Model parameters
$\theta$ are initialized either randomly (scratch condition) or from a
pre-trained state $\theta_0$ obtained by training on the source domain.
The optimizer state is not reset between tasks. Task~A training runs
for $E$ epochs on the Fashion-MNIST binary subset; Task~A accuracy
$\text{Acc}_A^\text{init}$ is recorded immediately after this phase.
Task~B training then runs for $E$ epochs on the MNIST binary subset.
After Task~B training completes, the model is evaluated again on
Task~A to obtain $\text{Acc}_A^\text{final}$. The forgetting drop is:

\begin{equation}
  \Delta_A = \text{Acc}_A^\text{init} - \text{Acc}_A^\text{final},
  \label{eq:forgetting}
\end{equation}

where $\text{Acc}_A^\text{init}$ is the Task~A accuracy after Task~A
training and $\text{Acc}_A^\text{final}$ is the Task~A accuracy after
Task~B training has updated the same parameters. A lower $\Delta_A$
indicates greater resistance to sequential interference.

\subsection{Noise Model}

To evaluate the robustness of the results under realistic NISQ
conditions, a hardware-calibrated noise model is applied using the
\texttt{default.mixed} density-matrix backend of
PennyLane~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane}. Three noise channels are
inserted after the variational block of each circuit, applied
independently to each qubit.

Amplitude damping with parameter $\gamma_\text{AD}$ models $T_1$
longitudinal relaxation:

\begin{equation}
  \gamma_\text{AD} = 1 - e^{-t_g / T_1},
  \label{eq:ad}
\end{equation}

where $t_g$ is the single-qubit gate time and $T_1$ is the $T_1$
relaxation time.

Phase damping with parameter $\gamma_\text{PD}$ models pure dephasing
beyond the $T_1$ contribution:

\begin{equation}
  \gamma_\text{PD} = 1 - \exp\!\left(-t_g \left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{2T_1}\right)\right),
  \label{eq:pd}
\end{equation}

where $T_2$ is the $T_2$ transverse relaxation time. A depolarizing
channel with rate $p$ is also applied to model residual gate errors
not captured by the relaxation channels.

Hardware calibration values are taken from IBM~Heron~r2 (April 2025),
summarized in Table~\ref{tab:noise}. The resulting noise rates at the
single-qubit gate level are $\gamma_\text{AD} = 1.28 \times 10^{-4}$
and $\gamma_\text{PD} = 6.40 \times 10^{-5}$, both well below the
single-gate threshold. The dominant observable effect in a 4-qubit
circuit is the cumulative readout error and the depolarizing channel
after multi-qubit entangling gates.

\section{Results}

This section reports results for two experiments: a topology ablation
comparing the three ans\"{a}tze under both noise conditions
(Section~\ref{sec:topology}), and a cross-domain pre-training
comparison evaluating three initialization strategies
(Section~\ref{sec:crossdomain}). All experiments are executed on the
Hercules HPC cluster of CICA with five random seeds; results are
reported as mean over seeds.

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

Table~\ref{tab:datasets} summarizes the three dataset splits used across
all experiments. The synthetic source domain consists of two isotropic
Gaussian clusters in $\mathbb{R}^4$ generated with \texttt{make\_classification},
separated by a margin of 3.0 along the first principal axis.
Fashion-MNIST~\cite{xiao2017fashion} classes 0 (T-shirt) and 1 (Trouser)
form Task~A. MNIST~\cite{lecun1998gradient} classes 2 (digit two) and
3 (digit three) form Task~B. Both image datasets are reduced to four
PCA components fitted exclusively on the training split of each task,
and then scaled to $[0, \pi]$ for angle embedding.

\input{tables/tab_datasets.tex}

The synthetic domain is constructed to have no semantic overlap with the
image tasks. Its classification boundary is a hyperplane in a
low-dimensional Gaussian space, while the image tasks encode texture and
shape information from pixel values processed through PCA. The MobileNet-V2
source, used only in Experiment~2, extracts 1280-dimensional feature
embeddings from CIFAR-10 images of classes 2 and 3 (bird and cat),
which are then reduced to four features by PCA.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

Three accuracy values are recorded per run. $\text{Acc}_A^\text{init}$
is the Task~A test accuracy after Task~A training completes.
$\text{Acc}_B$ is the Task~B test accuracy after Task~B training
completes. $\text{Acc}_A^\text{final}$ is the Task~A test accuracy
re-evaluated after Task~B training, without any re-training on Task~A.
The forgetting drop $\Delta_A$ is computed as in
Eq.~(\ref{eq:forgetting}).

Binary accuracy over a test set of $N$ samples is defined as:

\begin{equation}
  \text{Acc} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \mathbf{1}\bigl(\hat{y}_i = y_i\bigr),
  \label{eq:acc}
\end{equation}

where $y_i$ is the true class label and $\hat{y}_i$ is the predicted
class obtained by thresholding the circuit expectation value:
$\hat{y}_i = \mathbf{1}(\langle Z_0 \rangle > 0)$.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

All experiments use $n = 4$ qubits, $L = 2$ layers for SEL and Basic
Entangler, an Adam optimizer with learning rate $\eta = 0.05$, and
$E = 10$ training epochs per task. The batch size is 32 for all splits.
Source domain pre-training also runs for 10 epochs. Noisy simulations
use the \texttt{default.mixed} backend with \texttt{diff\_method=backprop};
ideal simulations use \texttt{default.qubit} with
\texttt{diff\_method=adjoint}.

The software stack is: Python~3.10.14, PennyLane~0.40.0,
PennyLane-Lightning~0.40.0, PyTorch~2.2.2, scikit-learn~1.4.2,
NumPy~1.26.4, torchvision~0.17.2. All jobs run on the Hercules HPC
cluster (CICA, Seville) under SLURM, partition \texttt{standard}, with
4 CPU cores and 16~GB RAM per job. Each run corresponds to one seed and
one configuration; the total computation across 60 runs is approximately
8~hours.

\subsection{Result Discussion}
\label{sec:results}

\subsubsection{Experiment~1: Topology Comparison}
\label{sec:topology}

Table~\ref{tab:topology} reports $\text{Acc}_A^\text{init}$,
$\text{Acc}_A^\text{final}$, and $\Delta_A$ for the three ans\"{a}tze
under both noise conditions, using random initialization (scratch).
Figure~\ref{fig:ansatz_decay} shows the evolution of Task~A accuracy
across Task~B training epochs for each architecture.

\input{tables/tab_topology.tex}

Under ideal simulation, SEL achieves the highest
$\text{Acc}_A^\text{init}$ at 96.0\%, consistent with its expressibility
advantage over the other two architectures. However, SEL also produces
the largest $\Delta_A$ of 18.0 percentage points: its dense, all-to-all
entanglement allows Task~B gradients to propagate across the full
parameter tensor and overwrite Task~A representations globally. The
Basic Entangler reaches only 75.0\% on Task~A, reflecting its lower
representational capacity with 8 parameters, but the restricted
connectivity also limits the reach of Task~B gradients, producing a
moderate $\Delta_A$ of 9.5 percentage points. TTN achieves 96.0\%
on Task~A and a $\Delta_A$ of 7.5 percentage points---the lowest of
the three---because its hierarchical structure confines parameter
interactions to local qubit pairs: gradients from Task~B update the
root layer without propagating to the leaf layers that encode Task~A
features.

Under IBM~Heron~r2 noise, $\text{Acc}_A^\text{init}$ drops by
6.9 points for SEL and 4.8 points for TTN relative to ideal simulation.
The greater noise sensitivity of SEL follows from the larger number of
two-qubit entangling gates per layer, each contributing depolarizing
and relaxation errors. TTN, with fewer and spatially local CNOT
operations, accumulates less decoherence per circuit run. The ordering
of $\Delta_A$ across architectures is preserved: SEL shows the highest
forgetting at 22.9 percentage points and TTN the lowest at 9.9. The
noise increase in $\Delta_A$ relative to ideal conditions is 4.9 points
for SEL and 2.4 points for TTN, which is consistent with the observation
that noisy circuits reduce the effective separation between tasks in
parameter space by blurring the loss landscape.

\subsubsection{Experiment~2: Cross-Domain Pre-Training}
\label{sec:crossdomain}

Table~\ref{tab:crossdomain} compares three initialization strategies
on the TTN ansatz: random initialization, synthetic Gaussian
pre-training (QTL-Synthetic), and MobileNet-V2 feature pre-training
(QTL-MobileNet). Figure~\ref{fig:convergence} shows the cross-entropy
loss during Task~A training for each strategy.

\input{tables/tab_crossdomain.tex}

Under ideal simulation, QTL-Synthetic reduces $\Delta_A$ from 7.5 to
5.5 percentage points, a relative reduction of 26.7\% with respect to
the scratch baseline. QTL-MobileNet produces an intermediate reduction
to 6.2 percentage points despite its higher Task~B accuracy of 92.5\%.
The fact that QTL-MobileNet performs better on Task~B but worse than
QTL-Synthetic on forgetting indicates that source accuracy alone does
not determine the degree of protection. The synthetic Gaussian source,
by training the circuit on a well-separated, low-noise binary
classification problem, places circuit parameters in a region of
parameter space with lower curvature, which is more resistant to large
gradient steps during Task~A and Task~B training. The MobileNet-V2
source, derived from a richer feature space, may drive parameters
toward a higher-curvature region that is better suited for visual
feature discrimination but less stable under sequential updates.

Under IBM~Heron~r2 noise, the absolute values of $\Delta_A$ increase
across all strategies. QTL-Synthetic still produces the lowest $\Delta_A$
at 7.9 percentage points, compared to 10.5 for the scratch baseline,
a relative reduction of 24.8\%. The gap between QTL-Synthetic and
QTL-MobileNet narrows from 0.7 to 1.2 percentage points under noise.
The convergence plot in Figure~\ref{fig:convergence} shows that
QTL-Synthetic reaches a lower Task~A loss at epoch 5 than random
initialization, confirming that the pre-training benefit on convergence
speed is preserved in both noise conditions.

\section{Conclusions}

This paper evaluated cross-domain pre-training as a strategy to reduce
catastrophic forgetting in sequential variational quantum classifiers.
Pre-training a VQC on a synthetic Gaussian source domain reduces the
forgetting drop on Task~A by more than 25\% relative to random
initialization in ideal simulation and by 24.8\% under IBM~Heron~r2
noise, without requiring replay buffers or parameter regularization.
The reduction does not require any semantic overlap between the source
and target domains; the benefit arises from placing circuit parameters
in a low-curvature region of parameter space prior to sequential task
training.

Among the three circuit architectures tested, the TTN ansatz produces
the lowest forgetting drop in all conditions. Its hierarchical parameter
structure limits gradient propagation to local qubit interactions,
which prevents Task~B updates from reaching the leaf-layer parameters
that encode Task~A features. SEL achieves higher initial accuracy due
to its expressibility but is the most susceptible to sequential
interference, with a forgetting drop 2.4 times larger than TTN under
ideal simulation.

The noise analysis shows that IBM~Heron~r2 hardware errors reduce
Task~A initial accuracy by 4.8--6.9 percentage points and increase
$\Delta_A$ by 2.4--4.9 percentage points relative to ideal simulation.
The relative ordering of architectures and initialization strategies
is preserved under noise, which validates the conclusions drawn from
ideal simulations in the context of near-term hardware. Closing the
remaining gap between simulation and physical device performance will
require hardware-specific mitigation methods such as zero-noise
extrapolation or probabilistic error cancellation, which represent
necessary extensions of this work.

Future work will extend the sequential task length beyond two tasks,
evaluate cross-domain pre-training with increasing qubit counts to
assess scalability beyond the 4-qubit regime, and investigate whether
structured synthetic sources---such as topological graphs or Fourier
modes---provide stronger protection than isotropic Gaussian clusters.
